A Bit of Background of Vietnamese Immigration

After the end of the Vietnam War, many Vietnamese sought refuge throughout the world. Two major waves of Vietnamese immigration occurred:
First Wave (1975)
This first wave of refugees were comprised of people who worked for/with the United States and/or the Southern Vietnamese government. These refugees were educated, often holding degrees in secondary education. These people were evacuated via helicopter alongside U.S. personnel and staff due to their risk of being targeted and held by the Viet Cong during their imminent capture of Saigon. They were more likely to be bilingual due to their education status and had an advantage when seeking asylum in other countries.
Second Wave (late 1970's-1980's)
The second wave of refugees comprised of mostly rural and working class people, with many being Chinese-Vietnamese. They did not want to live under the new regime, and sought asylum in neighboring South East Asian countries via boat. These boat rides were extremely dangerous, often being targeted by pirates or had a high risk of death due to drowning or dehydration. Most people did not speak another language.

Establishing the enclaves that gave way to Entertainment

The first wave of Vietnamese refugees in the U.S. had the favor of the government and were educated enough to establish the first Vietnamese enclaves and businesses, and sponsor friends and family to immigrate to the U.S. These first wave refugees also helped establish Vietnamese-language mass communication for the diaspora. This included radio, newspaper, television, and most relevant to this archive, entertainment.

The "Big Three" Vietnamese-diaspora entertainment companies are: Thúy Nga (best known for Paris By Night), Asia Entertainment, and Làng Văn. These companies were headquartered in Little Saigon in Orange County, California due to having the highest number of Vietnamese-diaspora in the world. They all created and produced variety shows, which are long live shows showcasing interviews, comedy sketches, one-act plays, and musical performances by both older and younger Vietnamese people. These companies not only created variety shows, but were also music labels for 1st (immigrants who immigrated in adulthood) and 1.5 (immigrants who immigrated during childhood) generation Vietnamese-American musicians.

The 1.5 generation of Vietnamese-American musicians under these labels helped establish community between younger 1.5 gen and 2nd gen Vietnamese, who often felt ostracized and underrepresented in mainstream media at the time. These musicians often perform and record covers of both Vietnamese and mainstream English songs to appeal to both older and younger audiences. These musicians were also more open to using the Internet and early social media (such as MySpace) to share their lives and communicate with their fans.

Why archive this niche?

I would be lying if I said my personal experience of being underrepresented where I grew up and being frustrated with my lack of connection to the Vietnamese-diaspora didn't lead me to pursue this rather niche topic. My younger self LOATHED the Vietnamese Bolero that droned from the TV from a bootleg DVD of Paris By Night sent by a relative. It was boring and I didn't understand a single lick of anything the singers sang (I've since warmed up to this genre, but it's not something that I'd listen to everyday).

My younger self lamented the peers who were too busy with academics to listen to music or say they listen to classical music because they played piano/violin. With the exception of the friends who shared my music tastes (Love you all dearly!), was there anyone out there who looked like me outside of Whatever, New Jersey that liked J-Pop? Or Euro-dance? Or just anything interesting at all?

One summer, I was visiting family in Southern California. I was a nosy child and decided to sneak into my older cousin's room just to see what she has there. I was greeted by a large poster of Trish Thuy Trang. What? A Vietnamese person who looks like she likes J-Pop and takes influence in Japanese pop culture? WHATTTTTTTT???? I remember searching her name on YouTube and finding the album that poster was from and looping one song, "Too Little Too Late" for hours afterwards.

This archive is a self-indulgent look into the culture I didn't grew up with but was curious about. Seeing how these musicians presented themselves on the early Internet outside the context of war felt refreshing. Even if it wasn't the best music (in my opinion), their presence gave me inspiration to pursue creative endeavors. I hope you have fun peeking into a niche, but important part of the history of the Vietnamese diaspora.
